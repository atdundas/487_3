Light Pollution and Population Change

Comparing light pollution trends in the top 10 growing and shrinking cities

A photo taken from the ISS while traveling above Africa, showing Sicily and much of Italy.

NASA Image and Video Gallery (29 July 2013)

There isn't much to see at night on a rural Kansas highway—less even than during the day. The sky is as big as ever, though, and dotted with stars innumerable as the crops which it sits above. Staring out the window as a kid, forehead against that glass, I watched as the car approached a glow, a glow which seemed to grow with every passing minute. I imagined it was a spaceship, hovering in the clouds, as unknown to us as we were to it.

But as the minutes passed and buildings began to appear, the glow moved overhead—no longer in the distance, but part of the atmosphere. The city was around us now, and the hazy sky turned orange. As much as I'd dreamt of starships, this was nothing odd. The heavens were in the countryside, I knew, but in cities hidden with an incandescent hue.

Population/Light Pollution Change

The chart below compares the percent change of population growth and shifts in light pollution for the the ten fastest growing statistical areas in the USA and the ten fastest shrinking. Population data is from the US Census Bureau, while light pollution data comes from Radiance Light Trends.

Data note: The data used in this story has been complied from the US Census Bureau and Radiance Light Trends. The light Pollution data is collected monthly and varies seasonally. For the purposes of comparison and to account for the occasional missing data points for certain months, radiance data for each year has been averaged into a single number.

The area of data selection from Radiance Light Trends is created by hand, so all light pollution data is from the general area of the town/city and does not accurately reflect city limits or other designators of place. The data comes from NASA's VIIRS DNB and the creators of Radiance Light Trends note that the satellite does not expressly collect light pollution data.

Additionally, VIIRS DNB does not capture data in the same range as human eyes. The creators of Radiance Light Trends state, "The satellite data is therefore less sensitive to white light than the human eye, and has sensitivity at infrared wavelengths that the human eye cannot see. For this reason, when communities switch from orange high pressure sodium lamps to white LED, the satellites often report a drop in brightness even if a human would say that the white LEDs look brighter."

Growing Cities

The graphs below compare light pollution trends to population growth in the ten fastest growing cities in the USA. The light pollution data used is a measurement of summed radiance. (More information on Radiance Light Trends.)

The charts show that generally as a cities population increase so does the amount of light pollution in its area. Prineville and Bend, Oregon show particularly large increases in light pollution. The percent growth of their radiance—respectively, 38% and 27%—outstripped their population growth. The only other area to do that among growing cities is Heber City, UT. Shrinking Cities

The data below shows that light pollution trends in shrinking cities are less clearly aligned with population change than in growing cities, though this fact is understandable on a couple fronts. Firstly, emigration out of a city is naturally less directly tied to location than immigration. New housing developments attract residents and expand a locations' light footprint, whereas departure could take place all across a city's residential areas. Secondly, cities are not likely to turn off the lights in area because it is less inhabited than it once was. Namely, if a street light is installed it will remain turned on.

The largest percent change in light pollution takes place in Pine Bluff, AR at 14%, followed by Mount Gay-Shamrock at -12%; both are Micropolitan Statistical Areas. In fact, eight of ten of the shrinking cities are considered micropolitan. Jump to discussion of micropolitan versus metropolitan areas.

Micropolitan, Metropolitan—East, West

All three of the fastest growing cities have at least one trait in common: either they are considered micropolitan areas and/or they are located in the west, where light pollution is less intense. To break that down, cities in the west are contributing light pollution to an area that is likely starting from zero instead of already being within the haze of another adjacent city. The micropolitan cities, Heber City and Prineville, their growth is likely to be outwards instead of upwards which requires lighting a large swath of land.
